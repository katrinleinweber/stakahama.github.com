Welcome

Condensed-phase species in the atmosphere pose a disproportionate impact on health and climate with respect to their mass budget, requiring further progress toward comprehensive characterization. Atmospheric particles (also often referred as aerosols in an atmospheric science context) are composed of sulfate, ammonium, nitrate, elemental carbon, organic compounds, trace metals, crustal elements, and water. These particles have both anthropogenic and biogenic sources (including industrial emission, electricity generation, various combustion processes, dust, forest, residential and wildfire burning, and other origins) and can be emitted directly in particle form (primary aerosol) or formed from condensation of low-volatility products generated from gas-phase reactions (secondary aerosol).

Both primary and secondary aerosols play a key role in climate and hydrology, civilian health, and transport of nutrient to natural ecosystems. For instance, mass concentrations of fine particles less than 2.5 micrometers in diameter have been associated with excess mortality, and are regulated for these health concerns. Atmospheric particles can also interact with solar radiation and either absorb or scatter radiation directly, or act as seeds for cloud-droplet formation, which also affects the energy balance of the planet. Our understanding of aerosol burdens and processes represent the largest source of uncertainty in the atmospheric system for predicting cooling and climate feedback effects. Improving our understanding of particle impacts on health and climate for present and future periods relies on a combination of modeling and measurement of aerosol emission or formation, transformation, and transport; including their interactions with the vapor phase. The work in this laboratory focuses on addressing these issues through the integration of various analytical and computational techniques.
